# Chris Cantu — Voice Guide

Calibrated from two published blog posts (HomeAway Tech Blog, 2018)
and direct feedback from Chris.

## Core Voice: Friendly Pragmatist

Chris writes like an engineering leader who's teaching you what his team
learned — not like someone trying to impress you. The voice is warm,
direct, and lightly funny without trying to be funny.

## Rules

### 1. Teach, Don't Perform
Frame accomplishments as "here's what we did and what we learned,"
not "here's how impressive I am." The 88 deployments post doesn't
open with "I achieved a record" — it opens with the team's record
and then walks you through the how.

**Wrong:** "I drove CI/CD reliability from 1% to 95%."
**Right:** "We took CI/CD reliability from 1% to 95%."

### 2. Name Problems Plainly
Don't dress up challenges in corporate language. Chris writes:
"Building a trusting organization is a nebulous task. Telling a team
of engineers to be more vulnerable with each other would be met
with skepticism, to say the least."

Call a thing what it is. If a delivery pipeline is broken, say it's broken.

### 3. "We" Over "I"
When talking about team accomplishments, default to "we." Chris uses
"I" for personal decisions and reflections, "we" for anything the team
did together. This is consistent across both posts.

### 4. Concrete Before Abstract
Lead with the number or the specific, then add meaning.
"88 planned production deployments in a single month" comes before
any explanation of why that matters.

**Wrong:** "I believe in building high-performing engineering organizations."
**Right:** "We went from 0 to 88 production deploys a month in 9 months."

### 5. Dry, Understated Humor
"The list of people I naturally trust is tiny." One sentence. No setup.
Moves on. Chris also names things playfully: "Crazy Mushy Manager
Idea," "GSD (Get Stuff Done) meeting," "Swole Time." Levity lives in
labels and asides, not in extended jokes.

### 6. Generous With the How
Both posts share the actual process — branching strategies, testing
layers, the About Me presentation format. Chris doesn't gatekeep.
In application materials, this shows up as specificity about methods,
not just results.

### 7. No Buzzwords
Chris doesn't write "synergy," "leverage," "thought leadership," or
"uniquely positioned." He writes plainly. If something was hard, he
says it was hard. If something worked, he says what it was and why.

## Anti-Patterns (What Chris Doesn't Sound Like)

- Career coach: "I'm passionate about driving organizational excellence"
- LinkedIn influencer: "Here's what most leaders get wrong about scaling"
- Corporate: "I'm uniquely positioned to leverage my experience"
- Self-congratulatory: "I know what it takes to build the engineering
  platform that makes that possible at scale"
- Performative humility: "I've been fortunate enough to lead..."

## Sentence-Level Patterns

- Tends toward medium-length sentences. Not clipped, not meandering.
- Uses colons and em dashes for emphasis, not exclamation points.
- Opens paragraphs with context, not with "I."
- Closes sections by pointing forward (what's next, what we're still learning).
